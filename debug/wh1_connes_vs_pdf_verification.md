## §2. Verbatim quotes for D1 through D7

### D1 — Definition of spectral truncation

**Source:** §3 introduction, lines 1058–1071 (page 18).

> "As we explained in the introduction, our goal is to extend this approach of
> geometry to cases where (possibly) only part of the spectrum of D is available.
> Most naturally, we may consider a spectral triple (A, H, D) where we take a cutoff
> of the operator D, described by means of a spectral projection P, projecting onto
> a finite number of the eigenvectors of D. Clearly, such an operator commutes with
> D and, in fact, any operator that commutes with D also commutes with P so that
> the truncation respects the group of isometries of the geometry. The operator D
> is thus replaced by P D = P DP = DP acting as an operator on the Hilbert space
> P H.
>
> Clearly, the *-algebra A does not act on P H any more. However, we may form
> the space P AP of operators which is invariant under the involution. In other
> words, P AP is an operator system in B(P H). We thus come to consider the triple
> (P AP, P H, P DP)."

The formal definition of *operator system spectral triple* is **Definition 3.1** (line
1085):

> "An operator system spectral triple is a triple (E, H, D) where E is a dense subspace
> of a (concrete) operator system Ē in B(H), H is a Hilbert space and D is a self-adjoint
> operator in H with compact resolvent and such that [D, T] is a bounded operator for
> all T ∈ E."

**Important nuance for round-1 Row 2:** Connes–vS do **not** use the symbol
$P_\Lambda = \chi_{[-\Lambda, \Lambda]}(D)$ in the body of this paper. The cutoff is
written simply as "P, projecting onto a finite number of the eigenvectors of D." For the
worked circle example (line 1113) it is written
"$P_n$ onto $\mathrm{span}_\mathbb{C}\{e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_n\}$" — i.e. an *index-truncated*
cutoff on the Fourier basis, not a $\chi_{[-\Lambda,\Lambda]}$ functional-calculus
projection. The continuous-$\Lambda$ formulation with the Borel functional calculus is in
*later* Connes-vS-lineage papers (Hekkelman–McDonald 2024, the UCP-maps paper), not in
the 2021 source.

### D2 — Definition of "operator system"

**Source:** Two definitions are given. The *concrete* one is **Definition 2.7** (line 391):

> "We say that a subspace E ⊂ B(H) is a (concrete) operator system if it is self-adjoint
> in the sense that E = E* where E* = {x* : x ∈ E} and contains the identity 1 in
> B(H)."

The *abstract* one is **Definition 2.5** (line 342):

> "An (abstract) operator system is given by a matrix-ordered *-vector space E with an
> order unit e such that for all n [the matrix unit $I_n = e \otimes \mathbb{1}_n$] is
> an Archimedean order unit for $M_n(E)$."

The motivating sentence in the introduction (lines 129–134) is the verbatim source for the
ResearchGate paragraph quoted in round 1:

> "Since P does not commute with A, this is not an algebra anymore. Moreover in many
> interesting examples if one takes the C*-algebra generated by P AP one gets a
> non-informative full matrix algebra. However, not all is lost: the space P AP is a
> *-closed subspace in B(H), i.e. it is a so-called operator system."

### D3 — Definition of propagation number

**Source:** §2.7, **Definition 2.39** (line 973), with the preceding setup (line 968):

> "Let E be an operator system. For an integer n > 0 one lets E^n be the norm closure
> of the linear span of products of ≤ n elements of E. It is an operator system."

> "**Definition 2.39.** The propagation number prop(E) of the operator system E is
> defined as the smallest integer n such that $i_E(E)^n \subset C^*_{env}(E)$ is a
> C*-algebra. When no such n exists one lets prop(E) = ∞."

Stability under stable equivalence is **Proposition 2.42** (line 1020):

> "The propagation number is invariant under stable equivalence, i.e., for any unital
> operator system E we have prop(E) = prop(E ⊗_min K) where K is the C*-algebra of
> compact operators."

(Stability under completely isometric, complete-order isomorphisms is **Proposition
2.41** at line 997.)

### D4 — Connes distance on operator systems / states

**Source:** §3, immediately after Definition 3.1, lines 1090–1098 (eq. (2)):

> "Since states are perfectly defined on operator systems, the above triple induces a
> (generalized) distance function on S(Ē) by setting
>
>   (2)   d(φ, ψ) = sup { |φ(x) − ψ(x)| : ‖[D, x]‖ ≤ 1 } .
>                       x ∈ E
>
> If E = A is a *-algebra then this reduces to the usual distance function [19] on the
> state space of the C*-algebra Ā. It also agrees with the definition of quantum metric
> spaces based on order-unit spaces given in [46, 37, 38]. It has been studied for
> truncations in [23, 32, 31]. The properties of this metric distance function and the
> notions of Gromov–Hausdorff convergence it gives rise to will be studied elsewhere."

Subtleties on what "states" means are clarified in §2.1.3 (line 417 onwards), where states
are defined as "positive linear functionals of norm 1" on the operator system itself —
not on the C*-envelope. The supremum in eq. (2) is restricted to $x \in E$ (the operator
system), not to $x \in C^*_{env}(E)$.

### D5 — Worked example: the circle

**Source:** §3.1, lines 1107–1136. The construction:

> "We consider C(S^1) ⊂ C(S^1) acting as bounded multiplication operators on L^2(S^1).
> An orthonormal basis of L^2(S^1) is given in terms of Fourier theory: $e_k(x) = e^{ikx}$
> for $k ∈ \mathbb{Z}$. These are of course eigenvectors for the Dirac operator −id/dx
> on the circle. We consider a spectral truncation defined by the orthogonal projection
> $P_n$ onto $\mathrm{span}_\mathbb{C}\{e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_n\}$ for some fixed n > 0. We
> will also write simply $P = P_n$. The space $P C(S^1) P$ is an operator system and an
> arbitrary element T = P f P in $C(S^1)^{(n)} = P C(S^1) P$ can be written as the
> following matrix with respect to the orthonormal basis $\mathrm{span}_\mathbb{C}\{e_k\}_{k=1}^n$ ...
> [n×n Toeplitz matrix shown explicitly] ...
> in terms of the Fourier coefficients $\{a_n\}_{n \in \mathbb{Z}}$ of $f \in C(S^1)$.
> Such matrices with constants along all diagonals are called Toeplitz matrices. Hence
> the spectral truncation $P_n C(S^1) P_n$ is given by the Toeplitz operator system of
> all n × n Toeplitz matrices."

The propagation number computation, **Proposition 4.2** (line 1621):

> "We have the following isomorphism of C*-algebras: $C^*_{env}(C(S^1)^{(n)}) = M_n(\mathbb{C})$.
> Moreover, independently of n one has $\mathrm{prop}(C(S^1)^{(n)}) = 2$."

(Two corrections to the round-1 inference: (a) the matrix is **n × n**, not
**(N+1) × (N+1)**; (b) the propagation number is **2 independent of n**, not "counts
bandwidth / scales with N". The bandwidth interpretation appears only in the dual
Fejér–Riesz operator system $C(\mathbb{Z})^{(n)}$, **Proposition 4.3** line 1681:
"The propagation number is infinite.")

### D6 — Isometry group preserved under spectral truncation

**Source:** §3, lines 1063–1065 and 1073–1080:

> "Clearly, such an operator [P] commutes with D and, in fact, any operator that
> commutes with D also commutes with P so that the truncation respects the group of
> isometries of the geometry."

> "The advantage of this spectral description is that we now have the possibility to
> work with a possibly finite-dimensional truncation of the spectral geometry, while
> keeping all the isometries of the original spectral triple intact. Indeed, the
> isometries of the latter are given by unitaries U such that $UDU^* = D$ and
> $U \bar A U^* = \bar A$. Now, since P commutes with such U, it follows that U also
> acts unitarily on PH while it maps PAP and D to itself."

Contrast with circulant matrices, **Outlook §6** lines 2611–2618:

> "The examples we have considered show that spectral truncations allows one to work
> with finite-dimensional operator systems, while keeping in tact the full symmetry
> of the original space. For instance, the Toeplitz operator systems possess an S^1
> symmetry, and as a consequence have a very rich extremal and pure state space
> structure. This is in contrast with the circulant matrices, where the symmetry is
> reduced to a discrete group. So, even though both spaces converge in Gromov–
> Hausdorff distance to the circle, for the second one loses a lot of structure in
> the finite-dimensional reduction."

### D7 — Recovery / Gromov–Hausdorff convergence theorem

**Source:** Connes–vS 2021 explicitly **defers** the GH-convergence statement to a
forthcoming paper. Three explicit deferrals:

Introduction, lines 217–221:

> "in §4.6 we analyse the distance function on the truncated circle and prove in Theorem
> 4.20 that it is larger than the Kantorovich distance of the corresponding probability
> measures on the circle. Other results in this direction have been reported in [23, 32,
> 31] while further Gromov–Hausdorff convergence results will be reported elsewhere by
> the second author."

§3 (line 1101):

> "The properties of this metric distance function and the notions of Gromov–Hausdorff
> convergence it gives rise to will be studied elsewhere."

Outlook, lines 2616–2618 (quoted under D6):

> "even though both spaces converge in Gromov–Hausdorff distance to the circle, for
> the second one loses a lot of structure"

The strongest *recovery-flavor* result inside the paper itself is **Theorem 4.20** (line
2262) which only relates Connes' distance to Kantorovich distance on the truncated
circle:

> "(i) The distance function on the state space S of the operator system $C(S^1)^{(n)}$
> is given for φ, ψ ∈ S and primitives Φ, Φ' = φ, Ψ, Ψ' = ψ by $d(φ, ψ) = \inf_{c \in \mathbb{R}}
> \|Φ − Ψ − c\|_∞$.
> (ii) The distance function on the state space S is larger than the Kantorovich
> distance $d_T(φ, ψ)$ of the associated probability measures on the circle."

There is **no** GH-convergence theorem stated in this paper — it is forwarded to van
Suijlekom's 2005.08544 followup and the subsequent Leimbach–vS 2024 / UCP-maps 2024
papers (which round 1 already cited).

## §4. Net verdict

**Round-1 claims hold with three corrections needed.**

The four CLEAN matches (D2 operator-system definition, D2 cont. C*-envelope collapse, D4
Connes distance, D6 isometry preservation, D7 attribution-of-GH-recovery-elsewhere) carry
the bulk of the round-1 mapping. The round-1 verdict on WH1 (ALIGN-WITH-CAVEATS) is not
overturned by anything found in the verbatim text. The most consequential round-1
finding — Gap 1, that GeoVac's $\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{GV}} = \mathbb{C}^N$ diagonal is
sitting at the C*-envelope side rather than at the operator-system side — is **directly
supported** by the verbatim text (line 130: C*-envelope is "non-informative full matrix
algebra").

**Three corrections that round 2 should adopt before building on round 1:**

1. **Drop the $P_\Lambda = \chi_{[-\Lambda,\Lambda]}(D)$ language for citations to
   Connes–vS 2021.** The 2021 paper uses index-truncation. The continuous-$\Lambda$
   Borel-functional-calculus form belongs to Hekkelman–McDonald 2024 and the UCP-maps
   paper. For GeoVac the index-truncation form ($P_{n_{\max}}$ onto Fock-shells $\le
   n_{\max}$) is the *exact* analog of Connes–vS's $P_n$ on the circle — this is actually
   a **better** match than round 1 stated, because the cutoff structure is identical
   (basis-index, not eigenvalue-window). The Camporesi–Higuchi monotonicity argument in
   round 1 Row 2 is then *unnecessary*.

2. **Revise the propagation-number reading.** The claim in round-1 Row 7 — that
   $\mathrm{prop}(\mathcal{O}_{n_{\max}})$ for GeoVac would scale with $n_{\max}$ "as
   $\mathrm{prop} \sim N$ for $N$-Toeplitz" — is **wrong**: the actual Toeplitz value is
   **prop = 2 independent of n** (Proposition 4.2). The round-2 prediction for GeoVac
   should therefore *not* be "scales with $n_{\max}$"; rather, the testable hypothesis is
   "is the GeoVac operator system propagation number bounded independent of $n_{\max}$,
   matching the Toeplitz S^1 behavior?" If yes, this is a stronger structural alignment
   than round 1 anticipated.

3. **Revise dimensions for the Toeplitz example.** Round 1 wrote
   "(N+1) × (N+1)"; the source uses "n × n" with n the basis-cutoff index. Cosmetic but
   should be fixed in any paper-bound writeup.

**Round 2 can proceed on the round-1 mapping with these three corrections.** No row of
the round-1 mapping table (other than the propagation-number scaling claim in Row 7) is
contradicted by the verbatim text. The central diagnosis (Gap 1: GeoVac is currently
sitting at the wrong representative — diagonal C*-envelope rather than the off-diagonal
operator system) is directly affirmed by the source. The recommended round-2 computation —
build $P_{n_{\max}} C^\infty(S^3) P_{n_{\max}}$ as the genuine operator system using the
already-computed Gaunt / hypergeometric Slater integrals, then compute $\mathrm{prop}$ and
the Connes distance — is unchanged in scope but should test against
prop = constant-in-$n_{\max}$ rather than prop ~ $n_{\max}$.
