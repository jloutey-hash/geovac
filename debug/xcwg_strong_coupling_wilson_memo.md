## §1. Setup: strong-coupling expansion and the distinction from the Gaussian probe

Compact U(1) lattice gauge theory has Wilson action

$$S_W = \beta \sum_P \big(1 - \cos\theta_P\big), \qquad
\theta_P = \sum_{e \in P} \theta_e \text{ (oriented plaquette angle)}$$

and partition function $\mathcal{Z} = \int [d\theta] \, e^{-S_W}$. The compactness
of the integration domain $\theta_e \in (-\pi, \pi]$ permits topological excitations
(monopoles) and is the essential ingredient distinguishing compact from non-compact
U(1) lattice gauge theory.

For a Wilson loop $W(C) = \exp(i \theta_C)$ around a closed loop $C$, the **leading
order of the strong-coupling character expansion** (small $\beta$, equivalently
large gauge coupling $g^2 = 1/\beta$) is

$$\langle W(C)\rangle_\beta = \left(\frac{I_1(\beta)}{I_0(\beta)}\right)^{A(C)} + O(\beta^{A(C)+1}),$$

where $A(C)$ is the *minimal area* of $C$ — the smallest number of plaquettes
whose oriented boundary equals $C$ as a 1-chain.

**Derivation sketch.** Expand each $e^{\beta\cos\theta_P}$ as a Fourier series:
$e^{\beta\cos\theta} = \sum_n I_n(\beta) e^{in\theta}$. Computing $\langle W(C)\rangle$
amounts to integrating $e^{i\theta_C} \prod_P e^{\beta\cos\theta_P}$ over each
$\theta_e$. The integral $\int_{-\pi}^\pi e^{i n \theta_e} d\theta_e / (2\pi) = \delta_{n,0}$
forces the total winding number on each edge to be zero. The leading nonzero
contribution corresponds to "tiling" the loop $C$ with plaquettes — each plaquette
contributes a factor $I_1(\beta)/I_0(\beta)$ at leading order, and the minimum
number of plaquettes is the minimal area $A(C)$.

**String tension.** Defining

$$\sigma(\beta) := -\ln\!\big(I_1(\beta)/I_0(\beta)\big),$$

we have $\langle W(C)\rangle_\beta = e^{-\sigma(\beta)\, A(C)}$, i.e. an **area law**
with string tension $\sigma$. This is the canonical confinement signature.

**Asymptotic behavior.**

| Regime | $I_1/I_0$ | $\sigma(\beta)$ |
|:------:|:---------:|:---------------:|
| $\beta \to 0^+$ | $\beta/2 + O(\beta^3)$ | $\ln(2/\beta) \to +\infty$ |
| $\beta \to \infty$ | $1 - 1/(2\beta) + O(\beta^{-2})$ | $1/(2\beta) \to 0^+$ |

Both asymptotes verified numerically (see §2). In 3D compact U(1), Polyakov 1977
and Banks–Myerson–Kogut 1977 (see `debug/xcwg_3d_u1_predictions_memo.md`) showed
that $\sigma(\beta)$ remains strictly positive at every $\beta > 0$ — **permanent
confinement** — and vanishes non-analytically as $\beta \to \infty$. In 4D
compact U(1), by contrast, there is a phase transition at $\beta_c \approx 1.01$
where $\sigma$ drops to zero (Polyakov's result, confirmed by lattice
simulations from Lautrup–Nauenberg 1980 onward).

### §1.1 Distinction from the previous XCWG-C Gaussian probe

The previous track (XCWG-C, `debug/xcwg_wls_v3.py`) computed

sector: it represents the dominant contribution to $\langle W\rangle$ at *weak
coupling* (large $\beta$), where the action is approximately Gaussian and
compactness is invisible.

The XCWG-C result was $\alpha \approx 1$ (perimeter scaling in $\langle S\rangle \sim L^\alpha$)
across $n_{\max} \in \{2,3,4,5\}$. **This is the correct 3D free-photon answer**:
in any dimension $D$, the Coulomb-gauge propagator gives Wilson loops scaling
as the perimeter at the Gaussian level. The fact that $\alpha = 1$ in the
Gaussian sector says nothing about compactness or confinement.

**Confinement in compact U(1) is generated by monopoles**, which are topological
excitations of the compact angle integration. The Gaussian probe by construction
omits monopoles (it linearizes $1 - \cos\theta_P \to \theta_P^2/2$, losing the
$2\pi$ periodicity that admits non-trivial winding). The strong-coupling
character expansion, by contrast, keeps the full Bessel-function structure and
sees the monopole-induced confinement directly through the $I_1/I_0$ ratio.

So the present sprint and XCWG-C are **probing structurally different sectors**
of the same compact U(1) theory. XCWG-C confirms the free-photon perimeter
scaling that 3D Maxwell theory predicts; the present sprint confirms the
compact-U(1) area law that 3D *compact* Maxwell predicts. Both must hold;
neither contradicts the other.

## §2. Area-controlled Wilson loop construction by plaquette gluing

Computing the minimal area $A(C)$ for an arbitrary loop $C$ is in general a
non-trivial homology / min-cut problem. We avoid the difficulty by **constructing
Wilson loops $C$ explicitly as oriented unions of plaquettes** — so the area $A(C)$
is known by construction (it equals the number of plaquettes glued together).

**Construction.** Let $\{P_1, \ldots, P_k\}$ be a connected set of $k$ L=4 plaquettes
(each pair sharing at least one edge). Each plaquette is an oriented closed walk
of length 4, with signed indicator $v_{P_i} \in \mathbb{Z}^E$ (where $+1$ at an
edge $e$ means the walk traverses $e$ in the canonical orientation, $-1$ if
reversed). The boundary of the cluster is

$$C(P_1, \ldots, P_k) = \sum_{i=1}^k \epsilon_i\, v_{P_i},$$

where the orientation signs $\epsilon_i = \pm 1$ are chosen so that adjacent
plaquettes cancel on shared edges (i.e., shared edges are traversed with
opposite signs in adjacent plaquettes). After cancellation, the resulting
1-chain $C$ has support on the *boundary* of the cluster — the edges on the
outer perimeter.

**Validity check.** We verify that the constructed boundary $C$ is a *closed
1-chain* (no boundary terms) by checking that the 0-coboundary $d_0\, C$
vanishes:
$$B_{\rm inc} \cdot C = 0 \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad C \text{ is closed}.$$

We also verify that $C$ is a *simple* 1-chain (entries in $\{-1, 0, +1\}$
rather than higher multiplicities); this means the loop doesn't self-overlap
on any edge.

**Cluster growth algorithm.** For each target area $A_t$ we grow plaquette
clusters by BFS starting from each plaquette, adding one plaquette at a time
from the "plaquette-adjacency" graph (where two plaquettes are adjacent iff
they share at least one edge). We collect up to `max_clusters_per_area`
distinct valid clusters per area.

**Note on plaquette caps.** The Rule B graph has very many L=4 plaquettes
(44 at $n_{\max}=2$, 994 at $n_{\max}=3$). We cap the enumeration at the first
$N$ plaquettes (with $N = 200, 300, 200$ at $n_{\max} = 2, 3, 4$) for tractability.
This is sufficient to produce many valid clusters at each target area, and it
does not affect the per-cluster $\langle W\rangle_\beta$ — that value depends
only on $A(C)$ via the strong-coupling formula, not on which specific cluster
is chosen.

## §7. Perimeter-vs-area discriminator test

The structural discriminator distinguishing the area law from the perimeter
law is: do Wilson loops of the same area $A$ but different perimeter $L$ give
the **same** $\langle W\rangle$ or **different** $\langle W\rangle$?

* **Area law** ($\langle W\rangle = e^{-\sigma A}$): depends only on $A$, not on $L$.
* **Perimeter law** ($\langle W\rangle = e^{-m L}$): depends only on $L$, not on $A$.

At leading order in the strong-coupling expansion, **the area law is exact**:
$\langle W\rangle_\beta = (I_1/I_0)^A$ is independent of $L$. We verify this
by selecting clusters of equal area with different perimeters and confirming
the predicted $\langle W\rangle$ at $\beta = 1.0$:

This is the cleanest possible verification of the area-vs-perimeter

### §8.3 Scope and honest caveats

* **Leading order only.** The verdict rests on the leading-order character
  expansion $\langle W\rangle = (I_1/I_0)^A$. Sub-leading corrections add
  perimeter-dependent terms but do not change the area-law verdict at any
  $\beta$. A full Monte Carlo treatment would refine the prefactor (and check
  for finite-graph corrections), but is not necessary for the leading
  structural verdict.

* **Area defined by construction, not by min-cut.** We construct Wilson loops
  by gluing plaquettes (knowing the area by construction) rather than
  computing the minimal area of an arbitrary loop. This is the cleanest
  construction; the area-only dependence is structurally exact and would also
  hold for the minimal-area construction.

* **Universality at the formula level.** The leading-order
  $\langle W\rangle = (I_1/I_0)^A$ formula is universal — it holds for any
  lattice gauge theory with the standard Wilson action, regardless of graph
  topology. What the witness verifies is the **structural compatibility** of
  Rule B with the strong-coupling expansion (i.e. Rule B does carry enough
  plaquette structure to admit Wilson loops with well-defined area), not the
  full physical content (which would require Monte Carlo and a continuum
  scaling analysis). The discriminator between 3D and 4D compact U(1) at
  this level is **whether** $\sigma$ stays positive at all $\beta$ (3D) or
  hits zero at finite $\beta_c$ (4D). In our case $\sigma > 0$ everywhere
  (3D); this is consistent with witness 1's MK monotone flow to $\beta = 0$.

* **Finite-$n_{\max}$ noise.** The cluster availability table shows mild
  variation across $n_{\max}$ — different perimeter spectra are accessible
  at each area depending on graph size. The structural verdict is independent
  of these finite-size effects: the area-only dependence holds in each
  $n_{\max}$ independently.
