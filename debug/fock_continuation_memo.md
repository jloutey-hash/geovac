## 0. Explorer's flag, engaged

The parallel literature survey at `debug/rh_literature_survey.md`
§3 states explicitly:

> "Hydrogen's SO(4) acts on S^3 = SO(4)/SO(3), **not** on H^3.
> There is no canonical Bianchi quotient attached to Coulomb.
> Selberg angle would require (a) Wick-rotating Fock's p_0 = i|p_0|
> (S^3 → H^3), or (b) arithmetic cover of Bargmann-Segal S^5 via
> SU(3,1). **No literature links SO(4) hydrogen to Bianchi
> directly.** Track RH-B not duplicated, but not well-motivated
> until the Wick step is justified."

This memo takes that flag seriously. The Wick step is mathematically
clean (§2) and a textbook result of Bander-Itzykson 1966. What the
literature survey correctly warns is that the Wick step alone does
not yield a **discrete** quotient — it yields H^3 with a **continuous**
SO(3,1) action. The leap from "continuous SO(3,1) symmetry group"
to "arithmetic subgroup Γ ⊂ SO(3,1)" is an additional step that
requires a separate selection principle, not provided by the Fock
projection itself. The remainder of this memo evaluates whether
GeoVac's internal structure (Rydberg constant, kinetic scale κ,
Hopf bundle, graph topology) provides that selection principle.

**Spoiler for the PM:** it does not. The verdict is **NO**.

---

## 1. Fock projection for bound states (E < 0)

Following Paper 7 §IV and Fock 1935:

Given a bound state with energy E < 0 and momentum p = (p_1, p_2, p_3)
in flat R^3, define the **energy-shell parameter**
$$p_0 = \sqrt{-2 m E}, \qquad p_0 > 0.$$
The stereographic projection onto the unit sphere S^3 ⊂ R^4 is
$$n_i = \frac{2 p_0 p_i}{p^2 + p_0^2}\quad(i=1,2,3), \qquad
n_4 = \frac{p^2 - p_0^2}{p^2 + p_0^2},$$
with $\sum_{i=1}^4 n_i^2 = 1$ (Paper 7 Eq. 5, verified symbolically
in Appendix A of Paper 7). The hydrogen Schrödinger equation in
momentum space, with the Fourier-transformed Coulomb kernel
$1/|p - p'|^2$, maps via the chordal identity
(Paper 7 Eq. 10) to the **free Laplacian on unit S^3**. The
symmetry group is $SO(4) = \text{Isom}(S^3)$ (Bargmann 1936).
Bound-state energies appear exclusively through the constraint
$p_0^2 = -2mE$ (Paper 7 Eq. 12). The full conformal extension to
SO(4,2) (Barut-Kleinert 1967) incorporates the spectrum-generating
algebra but does not change the positive-energy story below.

**What the GeoVac graph is.** Paper 0 / Paper 1: nodes labeled by
(n, l, m) with 1 ≤ n ≤ n_max; edges generated by the SU(2)×SU(1,1)
ladder operators L_± and T_±; graph Laplacian L = D − A with
κ = −1/16. Paper 7 §III proves that the continuum limit is the
unit S^3 Laplace–Beltrami operator. The discrete momentum spectrum
$p_0 = Z/n$ comes from the Rydberg shell structure.

## 2. Analytic continuation to E > 0 (scattering / continuum)

**The Wick rotation.** For unbound states with E > 0, set
$$p_0 = i \kappa_{\mathrm{phys}}, \qquad \kappa_{\mathrm{phys}} = \sqrt{2 m E} > 0.$$
(We use $\kappa_{\mathrm{phys}}$ here to distinguish from GeoVac's
kinetic constant κ = −1/16; no relation.) The energy-shell constraint
$p_0^2 = -2mE$ is satisfied as $-\kappa_{\mathrm{phys}}^2 = -2mE$,
consistent with E > 0.

Substituting into the unit-sphere identity (Paper 7 Eq. 5) gives
$$\sum_{i=1}^3 n_i^2 - n_4^2 = -1, \qquad n_4 > 0,$$
i.e. the target manifold rotates from the round S^3 ⊂ R^4 to the
**upper unit pseudosphere in R^(3,1)**, which is **hyperbolic
3-space H^3** in its Minkowski-embedded form. The metric
Paper 7 Eq. 11 picks up a minus sign,
$$ds^2_{H^3} = \frac{4\, du^2}{(1 - u^2)^2}, \qquad u = p / \kappa_{\mathrm{phys}},$$
which is the Poincaré ball metric on H^3 (where |u| < 1 before
the analytic continuation and the standard principal-series
analysis applies).

**Symmetry group.** The isometry group rotates from compact SO(4)
to the non-compact Lorentz group **SO(3,1)**. Specifically, the
two SO(4) Casimirs $L^2 = l(l+1)$ and $\mathbf{A}^2$
(Runge–Lenz squared) become the two Casimirs of SO(3,1), and
the discrete bound-state SO(4) representations of dimension $n^2$
continue to the SO(3,1) principal/complementary series (labeled
by a continuous parameter $s \in \mathbb{R}$ or $s \in i\mathbb{R}$
depending on sector). This is the standard continuum treatment of
Coulomb scattering; see **Bander & Itzykson, Rev. Mod. Phys. 38,
330 (1966)** §IV ("Group theory of the hydrogen atom. II. The
continuous spectrum"). The Coulomb scattering amplitudes are
matrix elements in the principal series of SO(3,1).

**Key structural fact.** The Wick rotation S^3 → H^3 is natural and
unambiguous for the continuum wavefunctions. It inherits a
**continuous** SO(3,1) action. No arithmetic or discrete structure
is introduced by the Wick step alone. This is the first part of
the Explorer's warning realized concretely.

## 3. Fate of the discrete graph under the Wick rotation

The GeoVac discrete graph at finite n_max is constructed **only from
bound states**: nodes $(n, l, m)$ with $1 \le n \le n_{\max}$.
The momentum-space image of each node is a 2-sphere in R^3 at
fixed $p_0 = Z/n$ — a discrete set of radii. Under the Wick
rotation $p_0 = i\kappa_{\mathrm{phys}}$, these discrete momentum
shells move onto the imaginary axis, and the graph **as
constructed** does not have a continuation: bound-state radii have
no scattering-state analog because the scattering spectrum is
continuous.

The possible analogs are:

1. **Bound-state graph with analytically-continued matrix
   elements.** The graph topology (node/edge structure) is a
   combinatorial object independent of $p_0$. Only the kinetic
   scale κ = −1/16 and the edge weights involving $1/(n_i n_j)$
   carry energy units. Analytically continuing the matrix elements
   gives a valid Hamiltonian on H^3, but the **nodes are still the
   bound states of the original compact problem**. This is
   structurally a Kato–Rellich continuation, not a new graph.

2. **Continuum graph with delta-normalized states.** Replace the
   discrete T_± ladders by continuous SU(1,1) raising/lowering on
   the principal series. The result is **not a graph** in the
   finite-combinatorial sense — it is an unbounded operator on a
   continuous spectral measure, and the Ihara zeta is not defined.

3. **Periodic quotient by a discrete Γ ⊂ SO(3,1).** This is the
   only route that produces a graph-like discrete object from H^3.
   **But Γ is not supplied by the analytic continuation.** It has
   to be chosen by an external selection principle — the content
   of question 4 below.

**Verdict on question 3.** The discrete GeoVac graph does NOT lift
to a continuous family on H^3 automatically. It either stays
discrete-in-bound-states (trivial continuation, no new content) or
dissolves into continuous spectrum. A discrete lift to H^3 requires
additional input: a Γ.

## 4. Does GeoVac's own structure pick out a Γ ⊂ SO(3,1)?

I survey four candidates. All four fail; the fourth fails less
decisively than the others.

### 4.1 Weyl group of SO(4)

The Weyl group of SO(4) is $W(D_2) = \mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/2$
(since the root system D_2 is reducible into two A_1 factors).
This is the discrete group of hydrogen's algebraic degeneracies
(n² degeneracy per shell). Under the Wick rotation, SO(4) → SO(3,1)
and the two compact A_1 factors rotate into one compact SU(2) and
one non-compact SL(2,R) / SU(1,1). The Weyl group of this real form
is again $\mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/2$ — a finite discrete
group, but **of order 4, not a lattice in SO(3,1)**. Acting on H^3,
it has fixed points everywhere and does not produce a discrete
hyperbolic quotient. **Dead-end.**

### 4.2 Hopf S^1 fiber

The Hopf fibration $S^1 \hookrightarrow S^3 \to S^2$ is central to
Paper 2 (fine-structure constant conjecture) and Paper 25 (gauge
structure). Under Wick rotation, the compact S^1 fiber remains
S^1 **if** one uses the non-compact real form that still has a
compact maximal torus, but the Hopf bundle over H^3 is not a
natural object: H^3 has no Hopf-type principal bundle structure
with a compact S^1 fiber. The non-compact analog would be the
**Lorentz twist bundle** $\mathbb{R} \to AdS_3 \to H^2$, but
AdS_3 is Lorentzian, not Riemannian H^3. **The Hopf S^1 action is
structurally irrelevant for H^3.** (This agrees with Paper 24 §V
and Paper 25 §VII's finding that the Hopf U(1) gauge structure is
specific to S^3 and the Coulomb projection, not a universal
feature.)

### 4.3 Arithmetic Bianchi groups PSL(2, O_K) for $K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$

The Bianchi groups act discontinuously on H^3 as orientation-
preserving isometries. The five class-number-one cases are
$d \in \{1, 2, 3, 7, 11\}$. For a selection by GeoVac to be
natural, some numerical invariant of the framework (e.g., the
Rydberg constant $R_\infty$, the Fock momentum $p_0 = Z/n$,
κ = −1/16, B = 42, F = π²/6, Δ = 1/40, α) would have to
discriminate one d from the others.

**Test.** Among the class-number-one discriminants $\{1, 2, 3, 7, 11\}$
and the related first-irregular discriminant 5 (used by
Bianchi-Humbert), which — if any — matches a GeoVac invariant?

- $d = 1$ (Gaussian integers, Picard group PSL(2, ℤ[i])):
  simplest / most-studied; co-volume = Lobachevsky constant ≈ 0.305.
  Selects no GeoVac invariant.
- $d = 3$ (Eisenstein integers): has exceptional 6-fold symmetry
  (cube roots of unity). Paper 25's Hopf-charge decomposition had
  an integer "6" (3-fold × 2 charge parity) at some truncations,
  but this is a coincidence of small numbers.
- $d = 7, 11$: no known GeoVac numerical link.
- No match with Rydberg constant $R_\infty = 10973731.568\ldots$
  m^{-1} (dimensional, depends on $m_e, e, \hbar, c$ — all
  non-structural in the dimensionless GeoVac framework).
- No match with $p_0 = Z/n$: a rational number, not an algebraic
  integer in any imaginary quadratic ring.
- No match with κ = −1/16: a rational scalar, not an algebraic
  element.

**None of the GeoVac invariants select any specific d.** This is
decisive. The Bianchi groups are natural objects of number theory
— they are not natural objects of hydrogen.

### 4.4 Picard group PSL(2, ℤ[i])

As the d = 1 Bianchi group, Picard is the simplest. But "simplest"
is not a physics selection principle. The only GeoVac object with
natural ℤ[i] structure would be a graph whose node labels live in
ℤ[i] — which the paraboloid lattice (n, l, m) ∈ ℤ³ is not.
The S^5 Bargmann-Segal lattice has complex coordinates $z_1, z_2,
z_3$ but these are coordinates on $\mathbb{C}^3$, not the Gaussian
integers. **No natural Picard selection.**

## 5. Verdict

**NO: the analytic continuation does NOT produce a natural discrete
quotient H^3/Γ.** The Wick step is clean and gives H^3 with a
continuous SO(3,1) action, but this is a **continuous** object,
not a discrete graph. GeoVac supplies no selection principle for
a specific discrete subgroup Γ ⊂ SO(3,1) — neither the Weyl
group, nor the Hopf fiber, nor arithmetic Bianchi/Picard groups
emerge from any framework invariant. The Bianchi groups are
objects of number theory; the Fock projection is an object of
hydrogen; nothing in GeoVac connects them.

**What this teaches (the value of the null result).** The
universal/Coulomb-specific partition of Paper 24 is sharpened
one more level:

- S^3 (Coulomb, bound): natural Riemannian manifold, discrete
  spectrum, Hopf bundle, π-via-calibration.
- S^5 (HO): natural complex manifold, linear spectrum, U(1) Hopf,
  bit-exactly π-free.
- H^3 (Coulomb, continuum): natural pseudo-Riemannian manifold,
  **continuous** spectrum, **no natural discrete subgroup**.

The Selberg-zeta angle on a hyperbolic 3-manifold requires
**external arithmetic input** (the choice of Γ). It is not
contained in the Fock projection. Selberg-on-hydrogen is therefore
structurally different from graph-zeta-on-hydrogen (Track RH-A):
the former needs a discrete group from outside, the latter is
entirely determined by the GeoVac graph topology.

The positive content of Track RH-A (all four Hopf graphs
sub-Ramanujan) stands as the single concrete RH-adjacent GeoVac
result of this sprint.

**Recommendation for next sprint.** Do NOT open a Selberg-zeta
track on H^3/PSL(2, ℤ[i]) or any other Bianchi quotient. The
closest positive direction from the survey — **He-Jejjala-Minic
2022** (Dirichlet L-functions from QM on hyperbolic surfaces) —
uses H^2/Γ not H^3/Γ, and their Γ is selected by the L-function's
modular form, not by a physics framework. Their work does not
provide a GeoVac bridge.

**Productive alternative directions** (in order of how natural
they are to GeoVac):

1. **Graph-zeta extensions.** Compute the Ihara zeta of
   (a) the N_max = 5 Bargmann-Segal graph (330-dim Hashimoto,
   Paper-25 headline case) to finish the RH-A sweep, and
   (b) the Hopf-quotient S^2 graph (Paper 25 §III.B) to test
   whether the fiber-collapse zeros factor separately.
2. **Spin-ful Ihara zeta on the Dirac-S^3 graph** (Tier-3
   infrastructure is already in place). Dirac edges differ from
   Schrödinger edges, so this is genuinely new and is the
   natural spinor extension of RH-A.
3. **Arithmetic from outside.** If the PI wants to explore
   arithmetic quotients, the honest framing is: "GeoVac provides
   a natural framework for graph-zeta questions; arithmetic
   quotients of H^3 are a separate research program we are not
   positioned to contribute to." This is a scope-boundary
   statement for Paper 29, not a research direction.

## 6. Closing

The Explorer's flag was correct. Track RH-B, as originally plan-
stated, is not duplicated in the literature but not because the
Bianchi/Coulomb connection is a missed opportunity — rather
because no connection exists to be found. The absence is structural,
as concretely as Paper 24's Coulomb/HO asymmetry is structural.
A one-paragraph version of this finding belongs in any future
Paper 29 §III (scope boundary of the RH program) or §VII (open
questions).

The door is closed cleanly. The null is valuable because it
redirects the sprint toward graph-zeta work (RH-A extensions, and
the spin-ful Dirac-S^3 Ihara zeta), which **is** inside GeoVac's
natural structural scope.
