\begin{abstract}
We show that qubit Hamiltonians derived from spectral graph theory lattices
exhibit structurally favorable Pauli decompositions for quantum simulation.
After Jordan--Wigner transformation, the GeoVac lattice Hamiltonian yields
$O(Q^{3.15})$ Pauli string terms in the number of qubits~$Q$, compared
to $O(Q^{4+})$ for conventional Gaussian-basis Hamiltonians
(published molecular data: $Q^{4.25}$ for LiH, $Q^{3.92}$ for
H$_2$O~\cite{trenev2025}).  The advantage originates in angular momentum
selection rules---enforced by Gaunt integrals at the two-electron integral
level---which cause the electron repulsion integral (ERI) tensor density
to decay as $\sim 1/M^2$ with the number of spatial orbitals~$M$, while
Gaussian ERI tensors remain near-dense at $O(1)$.
Beyond term counts, we quantify two operationally relevant cost metrics:
the number of qubitwise-commuting (QWC) measurement groups scales as
$O(Q^{3.36})$, and the Pauli 1-norm
$\lambda = \sum_i |c_i|$---which controls both Trotter step counts and
qubitization query complexity---scales as $O(Q^{1.69})$ with
$R^2 = 0.997$.  The sub-quadratic 1-norm scaling is the key result
for fault-tolerant simulation: it implies that Trotter circuit depth
grows far more slowly with system size than term counts alone would
suggest.
All Gaussian baselines are validated against published integral values
(Szabo and Ostlund for H$_2$; analytical STO for He).

For composed multi-center systems, the
pseudopotential approximation of the composed natural geometry
framework~\cite{loutey_paper17} yields block-diagonal electron
repulsion integrals with a universal Pauli scaling of $O(Q^{2.5})$,
independent of molecular topology (exponent spread~$0.02$ across three
molecules).  At equal qubit counts, this represents two or more
orders of magnitude fewer Pauli terms than published Gaussian
baselines~\cite{trenev2025}---an advantage that grows with system size
due to the $\sim 1.5$ exponent gap.  Across all 30~molecules in the
library (He through C$_2$H$_6$, spanning $Z = 1$--36, including
transition metal hydrides ScH and TiH), the composed
Pauli count is exactly linear in the qubit count:
$N_{\mathrm{Pauli}} \approx 11.11 \times Q$, with d-orbital blocks
producing a \emph{lower} coefficient (9.27) due to more restrictive
Gaunt selection rules at higher angular momentum.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ========================================================================

Practical quantum chemistry on near-term quantum computers is fundamentally
constrained by gate count.  For variational quantum eigensolvers (VQE) and
Hamiltonian simulation via Trotterization, the number of distinct Pauli
string terms in the qubit Hamiltonian directly determines the circuit depth
and measurement overhead~\cite{mcardle2020,cao2019}.  Reducing the Pauli
term count is therefore a central objective for quantum computational
chemistry.

Prior approaches to Pauli count reduction operate as post-hoc optimizations
on an already-constructed qubit Hamiltonian: qubit tapering exploits
symmetries to eliminate qubits~\cite{bravyi2017}; Pauli grouping strategies
reduce the number of distinct measurement circuits by identifying
simultaneously measurable sets~\cite{verteletskyi2020,yen2020}; and
low-rank factorizations of the two-electron integral tensor can compress
the operator representation~\cite{motta2021,lee2021}.  These techniques
are effective but treat the sparsity structure of the underlying
second-quantized Hamiltonian as given.

In this work we show that a different choice of \emph{basis}---the
spectral graph theory lattice of the GeoVac framework---produces
Hamiltonians that are structurally sparse \emph{before} any compilation
optimization.  The GeoVac lattice encodes electronic structure on a
discrete graph whose nodes carry hydrogenic quantum numbers
$(n, l, m)$~\cite{loutey_paper7,loutey_paper13}.  The key structural
consequence is that the two-electron repulsion integrals (ERIs) inherit
angular momentum selection rules from the underlying Gaunt
integrals~\cite{gaunt1929}, which zero out most of the $M^4$ possible
ERI tensor entries.  As the basis grows, the ERI density decays as
$\sim 1/M^2$, in stark contrast to Gaussian bases where the ERI tensor
is nearly dense.

Under the Jordan--Wigner transformation~\cite{jordan1928,bravyi2002},
each nonzero two-body operator
$a^\dagger_p a^\dagger_q a_s a_r$ maps to a bounded number of Pauli
string terms---typically 4 for density-type integrals ($p{=}s$, $q{=}r$)
and 8 for exchange-type integrals (four distinct indices), independent
of~$Q$.  (The JW $Z$-chains increase the \emph{weight} of each string
but not the \emph{number} of distinct strings per integral.)  Similarly,
each one-body term $a^\dagger_p a_q$ generates $O(1)$ Pauli strings.
The total Pauli count therefore scales as
\begin{equation}
  N_{\mathrm{Pauli}} \sim C_1 \cdot n_{\mathrm{h1}}
  + C_2 \cdot n_{\mathrm{ERI}},
  \label{eq:pauli_scaling}
\end{equation}
where $C_1, C_2$ are $O(1)$ constants and $n_{\mathrm{h1}}$,
$n_{\mathrm{ERI}}$ are the numbers of nonzero one- and two-electron
integrals.  For Gaussian bases with
$n_{\mathrm{ERI}} \sim M^4$ and $Q = 2M$, the two-body term dominates at
$O(Q^{4})$.  For the GeoVac lattice with
$n_{\mathrm{ERI}} \sim M^2$, the leading-order prediction is
$O(Q^2)$.  The empirical exponent of $\alpha \approx 3.15$ over the
range $Q = 10$--$110$ exceeds this asymptotic estimate, reflecting
spin-channel multiplicity (each spatial ERI generates up to four
spin-orbital ERIs) and subleading combinatorial terms at moderate~$Q$.
The scaling \emph{gap} between GeoVac and Gaussian encodings---roughly
$1$--$1.5$ in exponent---is robust regardless of the per-integral
counting and is the structural consequence of selection-rule sparsity.

We validate this scaling with systematic benchmarks on atoms (H, He) and
molecules (H$_2$) using the GeoVac lattice at increasing basis sizes
($n_{\max} = 2$--$5$), comparing against standard Gaussian basis sets
(STO-3G, 6-31G, cc-pVDZ).  All Jordan--Wigner transformations are
performed with OpenFermion~\cite{mcclean2020}.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{GeoVac lattice Hamiltonians}
\label{sec:geovac}

The GeoVac framework constructs electronic Hamiltonians on discrete graphs
whose nodes correspond to hydrogenic spin-orbitals $(n, l, m, \sigma)$
with $1 \le n \le n_{\max}$, $0 \le l < n$, $-l \le m \le l$, and
$\sigma \in \{\uparrow, \downarrow\}$.  The number of spatial orbitals is
$M = n_{\max}^2$; the number of qubits (spin-orbitals) is $Q = 2M$.

The one-electron Hamiltonian is a sparse graph Laplacian on this lattice,
with edges connecting angular neighbors ($m \leftrightarrow m \pm 1$) and
radial neighbors ($n \leftrightarrow n \pm 1$) within each $(l, m)$
shell~\cite{loutey_paper7}.  In the ``hybrid'' prescription, the diagonal
carries exact hydrogenic eigenvalues $-Z^2/(2n^2)$ while off-diagonal
elements come from the graph Laplacian scaled by the kinetic scale
factor $K = -1/16$.

The two-electron integrals use the full Slater--Condon framework with
Slater $F^k$ (direct) and $G^k$ (exchange) integrals, where the angular
coupling coefficients are Gaunt integrals computed via Wigner $3j$
symbols~\cite{loutey_paper13}:
\begin{equation}
  c^k(l_1, l_2, l_3) = (-1)^{m_1}
  \begin{pmatrix} l_1 & k & l_3 \\ -m_1 & q & m_3 \end{pmatrix}
  \begin{pmatrix} l_1 & k & l_3 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}.
  \label{eq:gaunt}
\end{equation}
The triangle inequality $|l_1 - l_3| \le k \le l_1 + l_3$ and parity
selection rule $l_1 + k + l_3 = \text{even}$ enforce strict sparsity on
the $c^k$ coefficients, which propagates directly into the ERI tensor.

\subsection{Jordan--Wigner transformation}
\label{sec:jw}

Given $Q$ spin-orbitals, the Jordan--Wigner mapping encodes fermionic
creation and annihilation operators as~\cite{jordan1928}
\begin{equation}
  a^\dagger_p = \frac{1}{2}(X_p - iY_p) \prod_{j<p} Z_j,
  \label{eq:jw}
\end{equation}
where $X_p$, $Y_p$, $Z_p$ are Pauli operators on qubit~$p$.  A one-body
term $h_{pq}\, a^\dagger_p a_q$ generates a Pauli string of weight up to
$|p - q| + 1$ (due to the $Z$-chain), while a two-body term
$v_{pqrs}\, a^\dagger_p a^\dagger_q a_s a_r$ generates strings of weight
up to $\max(|p-r|, |q-s|) + 2$.  The total number of distinct Pauli
strings after collecting terms is our primary metric.

\subsection{Comparison methodology}
\label{sec:comparison}

For the Gaussian baseline, we use published integral values from two
sources.  H$_2$ STO-3G integrals ($M = 2$) are from Szabo and
Ostlund~\cite{szabo1996} (Table~3.15, MO basis after restricted
Hartree--Fock); the FCI energy at $R = 1.4$~bohr is
$E_{\mathrm{FCI}} = -1.1373$~Ha.  He STO-3G integrals ($M = 1$) use
the analytical Slater-type orbital formulas with exponent
$\zeta = 1.6875$ from Hehre, Stewart, and Pople~\cite{hehre1969}:
$h_{11} = \zeta^2/2 - Z\zeta$ and $(11|11) = 5\zeta/8$, giving
$E = -2.848$~Ha.  These integrals are exact (not Gaussian fits) and
serve as genuine baselines for the Pauli count, measurement grouping,
and 1-norm comparisons.

For larger Gaussian bases (6-31G, cc-pVDZ), the Pauli term counts
reported in Table~\ref{tab:pauli} use synthetically generated integrals
with representative sparsity patterns, as PySCF was unavailable in our
build environment.  These are retained for scaling-fit purposes only;
the structural conclusion---Gaussian ERIs are near-dense while lattice
ERIs are selection-rule-sparse---is well established~\cite{helgaker2000}.

For GeoVac, we construct \texttt{LatticeIndex} objects with
\texttt{vee\_method='slater\_full'} and \texttt{h1\_method='hybrid'} at
$n_{\max} = 2, 3, 4, 5$, yielding $M = 5, 14, 30, 55$ spatial orbitals
and $Q = 10, 28, 60, 110$ qubits.  The helium atom ($Z = 2$, 2~electrons)
serves as the primary benchmark for scaling analysis.  For the molecular
comparison, we encode H$_2$ at $R = 1.4$~bohr using
\texttt{MolecularLatticeIndex} with $n_{\max} = 2$ per atom.

All Jordan--Wigner transformations use OpenFermion
1.7.1~\cite{mcclean2020}.  Pauli terms with coefficients below $10^{-12}$
are discarded.

  \label{fig:pauli_scaling}
\end{figure}

Power-law fits $N_{\mathrm{Pauli}} = a \cdot Q^\alpha$ in log--log space
yield:
\begin{align}
  \alpha_{\mathrm{GeoVac}}   &= 3.15, \quad R^2 = 0.9995. \label{eq:geovac_exp}
\end{align}
For the Gaussian baseline, published molecular Pauli counts from
Trenev~\textit{et al.}~\cite{trenev2025} (Jordan--Wigner with 2-qubit
reduction) give system-specific exponents: $\alpha = 4.25$ for LiH
($R^2 = 0.999$, 3~basis sets) and $\alpha = 3.92$ for H$_2$O
($R^2 > 0.999$, 3~basis sets).  These supersede our earlier estimate
of $\alpha_{\mathrm{Gaussian}} \approx 4.6$ from synthetic
integrals~\cite{helgaker2000} for the systems where published data is
available.

The GeoVac exponent reflects the $1/M^2$ ERI density decay,
which reduces the effective two-body contribution to $O(M^2 \cdot Q^2)
= O(Q^4/4)$ in leading order, with additional suppression from the
band-structured one-body graph Laplacian.

The scaling gap $\Delta\alpha \approx 1.0$--$1.5$ (depending on
molecule and Gaussian basis hierarchy) implies that the
GeoVac advantage grows with system size.

The density scales approximately as $\rho_{\mathrm{ERI}} \propto M^{-2}$,
which we can understand analytically.  The number of nonzero Gaunt
coefficients $c^k(l_1, l_2, l_3)$ is constrained by the triangle
inequality: for each pair $(l_1, l_3)$, only
$\min(2l_1, 2l_3) + 1 \le 2l_{\min} + 1$ values of $k$ contribute.
Since $l$ ranges up to $n_{\max} - 1$, the total number of allowed
$(l_1, l_2, l_3, k)$ quartets grows as $O(n_{\max}^3)$.  With
$M = n_{\max}^2$ spatial orbitals, this gives
$n_{\mathrm{ERI}} \sim O(n_{\max}^3 \cdot n_{\max}^2) = O(M^{5/2})$,
so $\rho_{\mathrm{ERI}} = n_{\mathrm{ERI}} / M^4 \sim M^{-3/2}$.
The empirical $M^{-2}$ decay is slightly steeper, likely due to additional
$m$-selection rules ($m_1 + m_2 = m_3 + m_4$) not captured in this
order-of-magnitude estimate.

\subsection{Measurement cost: QWC grouping}
\label{sec:measurement}

On near-term quantum hardware, the energy expectation value
$\langle H \rangle = \sum_i c_i \langle P_i \rangle$ is estimated by
measuring each Pauli string~$P_i$ independently.  Pauli strings that
qubitwise-commute (QWC)---i.e., share the same Pauli operator on every
qubit where both act non-trivially---can be measured simultaneously in
a single circuit~\cite{verteletskyi2020}.  The number of QWC groups
therefore determines the minimum number of distinct measurement circuits,
and reducing it lowers the total shot budget.

We partition each qubit Hamiltonian into QWC groups using greedy
sorted insertion (largest $|c_i|$ first), following the algorithm of
Verteletskyi, Yen, and Izmaylov~\cite{verteletskyi2020}.
Table~\ref{tab:qwc} reports the results.

A power-law fit over the GeoVac He data ($n_{\max} = 2$--$4$) gives
\begin{equation}
  N_{\mathrm{QWC}} \sim Q^{3.36}, \quad R^2 = 1.000,
  \label{eq:qwc_scaling}
\end{equation}
slightly steeper than the Pauli term exponent of 3.15 but well below
the Gaussian $O(Q^{4\text{--}5})$ regime.  The groups-per-term ratio
stays in the range 0.21--0.33, indicating that the selection-rule
sparsity translates directly into measurement savings: fewer terms
means proportionally fewer measurement circuits.

Note that the greedy QWC grouping is a heuristic upper bound; the
optimal minimum clique cover is NP-hard.  More sophisticated grouping
strategies (e.g., general commutativity or unitary
partitioning~\cite{yen2020}) would further reduce the group count, and
these improvements compose with the structural sparsity reported here.


\subsection{Simulation cost: Pauli 1-norm and Trotter bounds}
\label{sec:onenorm}

The Pauli 1-norm,
\begin{equation}
  \lambda = \sum_i |c_i|,
  \label{eq:onenorm}
\end{equation}
is the operationally most important cost metric for quantum
simulation~\cite{childs2021,campbell2019}.  For Trotterization, the
first-order error is bounded by
$\epsilon \le (t/r)^2 \lambda^2 / 2$, giving a minimum step count
\begin{equation}
  r \ge \frac{t\lambda}{\sqrt{2\epsilon}}.
  \label{eq:trotter_steps}
\end{equation}
For qubitization (linear combination of unitaries), the query complexity
scales as $O(\lambda t / \epsilon)$~\cite{childs2021}.  In both
paradigms, smaller~$\lambda$ means lower cost.

Table~\ref{tab:onenorm} reports $\lambda$, the normalized
$\lambda/Q$, and Trotter step counts at two precision targets:
$\epsilon = 10^{-3}$ (NISQ-era) and $\epsilon = 10^{-6}$
(fault-tolerant) for unit simulation time $t = 1$.

Power-law fits over the GeoVac He series yield the central quantitative
result of this work:
\begin{align}
  \lambda       &\sim Q^{1.69}, \quad R^2 = 0.997, \label{eq:onenorm_exp} \\
  r(10^{-3})    &\sim Q^{1.69}, \quad R^2 = 0.997, \label{eq:trotter3_exp} \\
  r(10^{-6})    &\sim Q^{1.69}, \quad R^2 = 0.997. \label{eq:trotter6_exp}
\end{align}
The identical exponents for $\lambda$ and~$r$ follow from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:trotter_steps}: for fixed~$t$ and~$\epsilon$,
$r \propto \lambda$.

The 1-norm exponent of~1.69 is dramatically sub-cubic, even though the
Pauli term count grows as $Q^{3.15}$.  The reason is coefficient
concentration: the largest terms (diagonal Coulomb and identity
contributions) dominate $\lambda$, while the vast majority of
off-diagonal Pauli strings carry small coefficients that contribute
many \emph{terms} but little \emph{weight}.

The genuine Gaussian baselines now enable equal-qubit 1-norm
comparisons (Table~\ref{tab:onenorm}).  At $Q = 10$, GeoVac
($\lambda = 11.29$) has $3.8\times$ lower 1-norm than Gaussian
cc-pVDZ ($\lambda = 42.95$), requiring $3.8\times$ fewer Trotter
steps.  At $Q = 28$, GeoVac ($\lambda = 78.36$) has $6.8\times$
lower 1-norm than Gaussian cc-pVTZ ($\lambda = 530.47$).  This
gap grows because the Gaussian 1-norm per qubit ($\lambda/Q$)
increases from 4.3 to 18.9, while GeoVac's grows more slowly (1.1 to
2.8).

For comparison, a dense Hamiltonian with $O(Q^4)$ nonzero ERIs of
comparable magnitude would have $\lambda \sim Q^{2+}$, giving
$Q^{0.3+}$ more Trotter steps at matched system size.  At
$Q = 110$, the GeoVac 1-norm of $\lambda = 657$ is already modest
enough for practical Trotter simulation with $r \approx 15{,}000$
steps at $\epsilon = 10^{-3}$.

As a control, the hydrogen atom (1~electron, no ERI) exhibits a
lower 1-norm exponent of~1.57, confirming that the two-electron
integrals contribute to---but do not dominate---the 1-norm growth.

\paragraph{Commutator-based bound.}
The $\lambda^2$ bound used above counts \emph{all} pairs of Pauli
terms, but two Pauli strings $P_j$ and $P_k$ that commute contribute
zero to the first-order Trotter error.  A tighter bound retains only
anticommuting pairs~\cite{childs2021}:
\begin{equation}
  \epsilon_{\mathrm{comm}} \le \frac{t^2}{r}
    \sum_{\substack{j < k \\ [P_j, P_k] \neq 0}} |c_j|\,|c_k|.
  \label{eq:comm_bound}
\end{equation}
Whether two $Q$-qubit Pauli strings commute or anticommute is
determined by their symplectic inner product: each Pauli operator is
encoded as a pair of $Q$-bit binary vectors $(x, z)$ via
$I \mapsto (0,0)$, $X \mapsto (1,0)$, $Y \mapsto (1,1)$,
$Z \mapsto (0,1)$, and the strings anticommute iff
\begin{equation}
  \sum_{q=1}^{Q} \bigl[ x_j^{(q)} z_k^{(q)} + z_j^{(q)} x_k^{(q)} \bigr]
  \equiv 1 \pmod{2}.
  \label{eq:symplectic}
\end{equation}
For $N$ Pauli terms, the full anticommutativity matrix is computed via
modular matrix arithmetic on two $N \times Q$ binary arrays at cost
$O(N^2 Q)$---no Hilbert-space matrices are constructed.  This is
feasible up to $N \sim 30{,}000$ ($n_{\max} = 4$).

%% ========================================================================
\section{Composed Systems}
\label{sec:composed}
%% ========================================================================

The structural sparsity demonstrated for single-geometry systems
(Secs.~\ref{sec:results}--\ref{sec:genuine_gaussian}) extends naturally
to multi-center molecules via the composed natural geometry
framework~\cite{loutey_paper17}.  We demonstrate this for four
systems of increasing complexity: H$_2$ (1~orbital block, the
single-block limit), LiH (3~blocks), BeH$_2$ (5~blocks), and H$_2$O
(7~blocks), using the pseudopotential approximation to decompose each
many-electron problem into coupled single-center blocks.


\subsection{Composition principle}
\label{sec:composition_principle}

The composed Hamiltonian partitions the orbital space into independent
single-center blocks, each retaining the angular quantum numbers and
Gaunt integral selection rules of the single-geometry case.  The
pseudopotential approximation (Ref.~\cite{loutey_paper17}, Sec.~II)
replaces explicit core-valence two-body Coulomb interaction with
one-body effective-$Z$ screening plus a Phillips--Kleinman repulsive
barrier, making the ERI tensor block-diagonal: cross-block ERIs are
exactly zero.

The block decomposition for each molecule is:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{H$_2$} (1~block): Single bond-pair block at $Z_{\mathrm{eff}} = 1$
  (2~electrons, no core, no Phillips--Kleinman barrier).  This is the
  single-block limit of the composed framework, structurally identical
  to the He encoding but at $Z = 1$ with R-dependent nuclear repulsion
  $V_{NN} = 1/R$.
\item \textbf{LiH} (3~blocks): Li core ($Z = 3$, 2~electrons) +
  Li-valence ($Z_{\mathrm{eff}} = 1$) + H-valence ($Z = 1$).
\item \textbf{BeH$_2$} (5~blocks): Be core ($Z = 4$, 2~electrons) +
  2~Be--H bond blocks, each containing Be-side
  ($Z_{\mathrm{eff}} = 2$) and H-side ($Z = 1$) orbitals.
\item \textbf{H$_2$O} (7~blocks): O core ($Z = 8$, 2~electrons) +
  2~O--H bond blocks (O-side $Z_{\mathrm{eff}} = 6$ + H-side
  $Z = 1$) + 2~lone pair blocks ($Z_{\mathrm{eff}} = 6$).
\end{itemize}
Each block is constructed from hydrogenic orbitals up to
$n_{\max}$, giving $M = n_{\max}^2$ spatial orbitals per block and
$Q = 2 \sum_b M_b$ total qubits.

with zero additional quantum circuits.  The operator decomposition
$H_{\mathrm{full}} = H_{\mathrm{elec}} + H_{\mathrm{PK}}$ is verified
to machine precision ($< 10^{-12}$) for all three composed systems,
and the algebraic identity
$E_{\mathrm{full}} = \langle\psi|H_{\mathrm{elec}}|\psi\rangle
+ \langle\psi|H_{\mathrm{PK}}|\psi\rangle$
holds with residual $< 10^{-13}$~Ha.

Hamiltonian 1-norm from $28{,}053$~Ha to $361$~Ha ($78\times$),
with no approximation.  The partitioned 1-norms across all composed
systems are comparable: LiH $33$~Ha, BeH$_2$ $355$~Ha, H$_2$O
$361$~Ha.

additional $4$--$5\times$ step reduction for composed systems at

\subsection{Cross-block ERI bound}
\label{sec:cross_block}

The pseudopotential approximation sets cross-block valence ERIs to
zero.  To bound the impact of this approximation, we count the number
of cross-block index quadruplets satisfying the Gaunt selection rules.

For two valence blocks with identical angular quantum numbers, the
cross-block ERI count equals the same-block count exactly, because
both blocks use the same hydrogenic orbital set.  Including
cross-block ERIs would at most double the ERI Pauli contribution.
Since $N_{\mathrm{ERI}}$ dominates $N_{\mathrm{h1}}$ at all basis
sizes, this would at most double the total Pauli count---shifting
the exponent by at most $\log 2 / \log Q_{\max} \approx 0.13$,
keeping it well below~3.0 even in the worst case.

Empirical verification (Track~CB, v2.0.37): for LiH at $n_{\max}=2$
($Q = 30$), the cross-block Coulomb ERI count is~130 versus~195
within-block (ratio~$2/3$), and the Pauli count with cross-block
ERIs included is~854 ($2.56\times$ the composed~334).  The $\le
2\times$ bound is slightly exceeded because the theoretical bound
counts only same-angular-set pairs, while the core block
($Z = 3$, max $l = 1$) has different radial extent than valence
($Z_{\mathrm{eff}} = 1$), producing additional nonzero quadruplets.
The 1-norm increases from~37.3 to~85.7~Ha ($2.30\times$), and QWC
groups from~21 to~88 ($4.19\times$).

Importantly, simply adding cross-block ERIs while removing the PK
pseudopotential does \emph{not} improve accuracy: a 4-electron FCI
test gives 29\% error (coupled) versus 15\% (composed with PK)
versus 10.9\% (no PK, no cross-block).  The root cause is that the
composed orbital basis uses different effective charges per block,
so cross-block ERIs add electron--electron repulsion without the
balancing cross-center nuclear attraction (one-body terms where
each orbital feels all nuclei).  This confirms that the
pseudopotential is not redundant in second quantization for the
composed basis: PK encodes a one-body effective potential that
approximates both core--valence repulsion and orthogonality
enforcement, and replacing it with explicit two-body terms is
incomplete without also adding two-center one-body terms.
The block-diagonal ERI structure thus remains the correct
operating point for the composed architecture.

\textit{Note added (v2.0.42).}
Paper~19~\cite{loutey_paper19} resolves the incompleteness identified above.
The ``balanced coupled'' Hamiltonian adds both cross-block ERIs
\emph{and} cross-center nuclear attraction integrals
$\langle \psi_{nlm}^A | V_B | \psi_{n'l'm'}^A \rangle$, computed
analytically via incomplete gamma functions (machine precision).
The multipole expansion of $V_{ne}$ terminates exactly at
$L_{\max} = 2 l_{\max}$ by Gaunt selection rules.  The resulting
Hamiltonian produces the only bound LiH configuration in
4-electron FCI (1.8\% energy error vs PK's 15.0\%) and achieves
0.20\% energy error at $n_{\max} = 3$ with analytical integrals.
The resource tradeoff is regime-dependent: for VQE/NISQ (Pauli-count
dominated), composed with PK remains cheaper; for QPE (1-norm
dominated), balanced coupled eliminates PK entirely, which is
decisive for heavy atoms where PK's $Z^2$ barrier dominates
the 1-norm.  See Sec.~\ref{sec:balanced_coupled} for the
resource census.


\subsection{Limitations of the composed-system analysis}
\label{sec:composed_limitations}

The comparison in this section quantifies quantum \emph{simulation cost}
(Pauli terms, measurement groups, 1-norm), not basis-set quality.
GeoVac uses hydrogenic spin-orbitals in natural geometry blocks while
Gaussian methods use contracted GTOs; the physical content per qubit
differs.  The metrics that determine quantum simulation cost---Pauli
term count, 1-norm, and qubit count---are independent of the classical
PES accuracy.  Several approximations enter the composed-system
results, and we document them below, distinguishing those that affect
simulation cost from those that affect only energy accuracy:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Cross-block ERIs set to zero:} The pseudopotential
  approximation eliminates all cross-block two-electron integrals.
  Selection-rule counting (Sec.~\ref{sec:cross_block}) bounds the
  impact at $\le 2\times$ the reported Pauli counts.
  \textit{Resolved:} Paper~19~\cite{loutey_paper19} includes all
  cross-block ERIs with Gaunt selection rules preserved across
  block boundaries.
\item \textbf{Cross-center nuclear attraction omitted:} One-body
  cross-center terms (e.g., H~orbital feeling the Li~nucleus) are
  not included.  These affect $N_{\mathrm{h1}}$ but not ERI scaling;
  at typical bond lengths the cross-center overlap decays
  exponentially.
  \textit{Resolved:} Paper~19 computes these integrals analytically
  via incomplete gamma functions.  33~terms at $n_{\max}=2$,
  168 at $n_{\max}=3$.
\item \textbf{Phillips--Kleinman parameters $Z^2$-scaled:} The
  repulsive barrier coefficients are $Z^2$-scaled from the
  Li$^{2+}$ core, not computed ab initio for each system.  This
  affects energy accuracy but not the Pauli count scaling.
\item \textbf{Valence $Z_{\mathrm{eff}}$ is constant:} The effective
  nuclear charge seen by valence electrons is position-independent,
  not $r$-dependent.  A self-consistent $Z_{\mathrm{eff}}(r)$ would
  change energies but not the orbital block structure.
\item \textbf{Gaussian comparison uses 2-qubit reduction:} The
  published Gaussian Pauli counts use a 2-qubit reduction
  (particle number and spin symmetry); GeoVac does not.  This
  makes the comparison conservative for GeoVac.
\item \textbf{$R_{\mathrm{eq}}$ error measures PES shape, not
  single-point quality:} The reported $R_{\mathrm{eq}}$ errors
  (5.3\% for LiH, 11.7\% for BeH$_2$, 26\% for H$_2$O) reflect
  the equilibrium distance of the composed PES, not the energy
  accuracy at any particular geometry.  For quantum simulation at
  a fixed nuclear configuration---the standard use case in
  fault-tolerant resource estimation~\cite{lee2021,reiher2017}---the
  relevant comparison is simulation cost at the same geometry and
  qubit count.
\item \textbf{Valence-valence coupling not captured:} The
  block-diagonal approximation eliminates inter-bond-pair electron
  interactions.  For BeH$_2$, this produces a qualitatively correct PES
  (bound state at $R_{\mathrm{eq}} = 3.31$~bohr) but quantitatively
  poor accuracy (32\% error vs.\ experiment).  Two correction approaches
  were tested: (i)~$Z_{\mathrm{eff}}$ partitioning (dividing screened
  charge among bonds) worsened $R_{\mathrm{eq}}$ to 103\% error;
  (ii)~classical point-charge inter-bond repulsion moved
  $R_{\mathrm{eq}}$ in the wrong direction.  The root cause is that bond
  pairs sharing a nucleus require orbital-level coupling (exchange and
  orthogonalization), not scalar energy corrections.  This limitation
  applies to the \emph{accuracy} of composed polyatomic PES, not to the
  Pauli term scaling, which remains valid as a lower bound.
  \textit{Partially resolved:} Paper~19 includes cross-block ERIs
  and cross-center $V_{ne}$ between all block pairs.  BeH$_2$
  4-electron FCI improves from 27.4\% (PK) to 10.7\% (balanced).
\end{enumerate}


\subsection{Transcorrelated variant}
\label{sec:tc_composed}

The electron-electron cusp ($1/r_{12}$ singularity) limits basis
convergence at each level of the natural geometry hierarchy
(Paper~18, Sec.~II).  The transcorrelated (TC) method removes
this singularity via a similarity transformation
$H_{\mathrm{TC}} = e^{-J} H\, e^{J}$ with Jastrow factor
$J = \tfrac{1}{2} r_{12}$.  For the composed qubit pipeline,
the TC transformation replaces the standard Coulomb ERI with a
gradient operator $G = -\tfrac{1}{2}\,\hat{r}_{12} \cdot
(\nabla_1 - \nabla_2)$, while leaving one-body integrals unchanged.
A constant shift of $-\tfrac{1}{4}$ per electron pair arises from
the Baker-Campbell-Hausdorff second-order term
$\tfrac{1}{2}|\nabla J|^2$.  The BCH expansion terminates exactly
at second order for two-electron blocks, and three-body terms vanish
between composed blocks (no shared electrons).

The TC Hamiltonian is non-Hermitian: the gradient operator $G$ breaks
the chemist symmetry $\langle pq|rs\rangle \ne \langle rs|pq\rangle$,
so the Jordan-Wigner transformation produces additional Pauli terms
from the antisymmetric part of the ERI.  However, the Gaunt angular
selection rules---which determine \emph{which} index quadruplets are
nonzero---are preserved, because $J = \tfrac{1}{2}r_{12}$ is
rotationally invariant.  The TC transformation densifies the radial
integrals within each allowed angular channel but does not create
new angular channels.

from 562 $\to$ 1{,}498 (LiH), 936 $\to$ 2{,}496 (BeH$_2$), and
1{,}310 $\to$ 3{,}494 (H$_2$O)---a uniform $2.67\times$ ratio---bringing
the total TC/standard ratio to $4.49\times$.  The angular gradient is
therefore a \emph{negative result for quantum efficiency}: the cost
exceeds the accuracy benefit by more than two orders of magnitude at the
composed operating point.  The radial-only TC Hamiltonian
(\texttt{include\_angular=False}) remains the default for the composed
pipeline.

A PES scan for LiH confirms that both the standard and TC Pauli
counts are $R$-independent (334 and 562 at all sampled bond lengths),
consistent with the selection-rule origin of the sparsity.

\subsection{Discussion of composed results}
\label{sec:composed_discussion}

The composed-geometry approach preserves and improves the structural
sparsity of single-geometry GeoVac Hamiltonians.  Three factors drive
this:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Block-diagonal ERIs:} The pseudopotential approximation
  eliminates all cross-block two-electron integrals, reducing the
  ERI count from $O(M^4)$ to $O(\sum_b M_b^4)$.
\item \textbf{Angular selection rules preserved:} Each block retains
  the Gaunt integral sparsity from the single-center case, with ERI
  density decreasing as $\sim 1/M_b^2$ within each block.
\item \textbf{Composition is additive:} Adding a new atomic center
  adds a new ERI block but does not create cross-block entries.
\end{enumerate}
The universality of the $Q^{2.5}$ exponent across LiH, BeH$_2$, and
H$_2$O---systems with 3, 5, and~7 orbital blocks,
respectively---is confirmed by a 28-molecule library spanning
$Z = 1$--36, including multi-center systems (LiF, CO, N$_2$, F$_2$,
NaCl, CH$_2$O, C$_2$H$_2$, C$_2$H$_6$) with up to 8~nuclei
(Sec.~\ref{sec:multi_center}).  Across all 28~molecules, the composed
Pauli count is exactly linear in~$Q$: $N_{\mathrm{Pauli}} \approx
11.11 \times Q$, confirming that the scaling advantage extends
to arbitrary polyatomic systems via the fiber bundle
decomposition~\cite{loutey_paper17}.

\subsection{Origin of structural sparsity}
\label{sec:origin}

The Pauli count advantage of the GeoVac lattice is not an artifact of
small system sizes or of comparing against minimal Gaussian bases.  It
arises from a fundamental structural difference in the two-electron
integral tensor.

In a Gaussian basis, the molecular orbital (MO) ERIs
$\langle pq | rs \rangle$ are obtained by a four-index transformation of
the atomic orbital (AO) integrals.  Since Gaussian product functions have
no angular quantum numbers in the MO basis, there are no selection rules
to zero out tensor entries.  The MO ERI tensor is generically dense,
with $O(M^4)$ nonzero entries.

In the GeoVac lattice, the spin-orbitals carry explicit angular momentum
labels $(l, m)$.  The two-electron Coulomb operator, expanded in Legendre
polynomials, couples orbitals through Gaunt integrals that enforce
\begin{equation}
  |l_a - l_c| \le k \le l_a + l_c, \quad
  l_a + k + l_c = \text{even},
\end{equation}
plus the magnetic selection rule $m_a - m_c = m_d - m_b$.  These
constraints eliminate the vast majority of the $M^4$ possible ERI entries.
The surviving entries grow as $O(M^2)$---a quadratic reduction in the
dominant cost term of Eq.~\eqref{eq:pauli_scaling}.

The $d$-orbital results of Sec.~\ref{sec:d_orbitals} demonstrate that
this sparsity \emph{increases} with angular momentum: $d$-$d$ ERI
density is 4.0\% versus 8.9\% for $s$+$p$, because the Gaunt triangle
inequality $|l_a - l_c| \le k \le l_a + l_c$ allows only $k = 0, 2, 4$
for $l = 2$, the same three multipole orders as $l = 1$ but distributed
over a larger angular space ($5 \times 5$ vs.\ $3 \times 3$ magnetic
quantum number grids).  This is the structural opposite of Gaussian
bases, where transition metal $d$-orbitals produce the densest ERI
tensors due to the absence of angular selection rules in the molecular
orbital basis.  For quantum simulation of strongly correlated $d$-electron
systems---transition metal complexes, catalytic intermediates---the
GeoVac lattice offers a structural sparsity advantage that grows with
the angular complexity of the system.

\subsection{Implications for quantum simulation}
\label{sec:trotter}

The results of Secs.~\ref{sec:measurement}--\ref{sec:onenorm}
show that all three cost metrics---term count, measurement groups,
and 1-norm---benefit from the selection-rule sparsity, but at
different rates.  For first-order Trotterization, the gate count per
step scales as $O(N_{\mathrm{Pauli}} \cdot \bar{w})$ where $\bar{w}$
is the average Pauli weight~\cite{childs2021}, but the
\emph{number of steps} is controlled by the 1-norm
(Eq.~\ref{eq:trotter_steps}).  Because $\lambda \sim Q^{1.69}$ grows
far more slowly than $N_{\mathrm{Pauli}} \sim Q^{3.15}$, the total
gate count is more favorable than a naive term-count analysis would
suggest.

For VQE on near-term hardware, the shot budget scales with the number
of QWC measurement groups (Sec.~\ref{sec:measurement}).  The
$O(Q^{3.36})$ group scaling, combined with the coefficient
concentration noted above, implies that the GeoVac lattice basis
provides compounding advantages: fewer circuits per iteration, smaller
variance per shot (from coefficient concentration), and fewer Trotter
steps if Hamiltonian simulation is used as a
subroutine~\cite{verteletskyi2020}.

For fault-tolerant algorithms based on qubitization or quantum signal
processing, the dominant cost is the number of queries to a
block-encoding of~$H$, which scales as $O(\lambda t / \epsilon)$ for
simulation time~$t$ and error~$\epsilon$~\cite{childs2021}.  The
$Q^{1.69}$ 1-norm scaling directly reduces this query count relative
to dense-basis encodings with $\lambda \sim Q^{2+}$.
The commutator-based analysis (Sec.~\ref{sec:onenorm}) confirms that
the advantage is structural: selection-rule sparsity reduces the
fraction of anticommuting Pauli term pairs ($\sim Q^{-0.45}$), not
just the coefficient magnitudes, yielding a tighter effective scaling
of $r \sim Q^{1.47}$ for first-order Trotterization.

\subsection{Connection to other sparse approaches}
\label{sec:related}

Several recent proposals exploit tensor structure to reduce quantum
chemistry resource requirements.  Double factorization~\cite{motta2021}
and tensor hypercontraction~\cite{lee2021} decompose the ERI tensor to
reduce the effective number of terms, achieving $O(M^2)$--$O(M^3)$ scaling
in the number of unique rotations.  Our approach is complementary: rather
than compressing a dense tensor after the fact, the GeoVac lattice
produces a natively sparse tensor.  The two strategies could in principle
be combined---applying low-rank factorization to an already-sparse ERI
tensor---though we leave this exploration to future work.

Qubit-ADAPT-VQE~\cite{tang2021} and hardware-efficient
ans\"atze~\cite{kandala2017} also aim to reduce circuit depth by limiting
the operator pool.  The GeoVac lattice provides a physically motivated
basis for such pools: only operators corresponding to nonzero Hamiltonian
terms need be included, naturally pruning the search space.

\subsection{Composability with existing reduction techniques}
\label{sec:composability}

A natural question is whether the structural sparsity reported here
composes with existing Pauli reduction techniques, or whether it
substitutes for them.  The answer is that these approaches operate on
orthogonal axes and their benefits are additive.

\emph{Qubit tapering}~\cite{bravyi2017} exploits $\mathbb{Z}_2$
symmetries to eliminate redundant qubits.  The GeoVac lattice
Hamiltonian preserves $S_z$ and particle-number symmetry, so the
same 2-qubit reduction available to Gaussian encodings applies.
Indeed, the Gaussian baselines from Trenev~\textit{et
al.}~\cite{trenev2025} already use this reduction; GeoVac's
advantages are reported \emph{without} it, making the comparisons
conservative.

\emph{Pauli grouping} (QWC, general commutativity, unitary
partitioning~\cite{verteletskyi2020,yen2020}) reduces measurement
overhead by identifying simultaneously measurable Pauli strings.
These algorithms take a set of Pauli strings as input and group them;
a smaller input set produces fewer groups.  GeoVac provides the
smaller input set---$Q^{3.15}$ instead of $Q^{4+}$ terms---so
grouping starts from a more favorable position.

\emph{Low-rank factorization}~\cite{motta2021,lee2021} decomposes the
ERI tensor to reduce the number of distinct rotation circuits.  The
rank of the GeoVac ERI tensor is bounded by the number of allowed
Gaunt channels, which grows as $O(l_{\max}^2)$---already much lower
than the $O(M^2)$ rank of a generic dense tensor.  Applying low-rank
factorization to a natively sparse tensor would start from a lower
rank, potentially achieving further compression.

In short, GeoVac's structural sparsity provides a better starting
point for every downstream optimization in the standard quantum
chemistry compilation pipeline.  The $Q^{2.5}$ composed scaling
(Sec.~\ref{sec:composed}) is a \emph{floor} that existing reduction
techniques can only improve upon.

\subsection{Fault-tolerant resource implications}
\label{sec:ft_resources}

For fault-tolerant quantum simulation, the total cost is controlled
by the product of gate count per Trotter step and the number of
steps~$r$.  We emphasize two structural advantages of the GeoVac
encoding in this regime.

First, the sub-quadratic 1-norm scaling ($\lambda \sim Q^{1.69}$)
means that the GeoVac advantage \emph{grows faster} for fault-tolerant
algorithms than for VQE.  The Trotter step count
$r \propto \lambda / \sqrt{\epsilon}$ depends on 1-norm, not term
count; the composed 1-norms (Table~\ref{tab:composed_onenorm}) show
that LiH at $Q = 84$ requires $r \approx 4{,}500$ steps at chemical
accuracy ($\epsilon = 10^{-3}$), while BeH$_2$ at $Q = 140$ requires
$r \approx 16{,}500$---modest counts for early fault-tolerant devices.

Second, the commutator-based bound (Table~\ref{tab:comm_bound})
provides a further $4$--$7\times$ step reduction that also grows
with system size ($r_{\mathrm{comm}} \sim Q^{1.47}$ vs.\
$r_{\lambda^2} \sim Q^{1.69}$).  This reflects the physical
mechanism: selection-rule sparsity ensures that an increasing fraction
of Pauli term pairs commute, reducing the effective error per step.

For qubitization~\cite{childs2021,lee2021}, the query complexity
scales as $O(\lambda t / \epsilon)$ with no quadratic dependence.
The $Q^{1.69}$ 1-norm scaling translates directly into a
$Q^{0.3\text{--}0.5}$ query-count advantage over encodings with
$\lambda \sim Q^{2+}$.  At $Q = 110$, this corresponds to a
$\sim 3\times$ reduction in queries; at $Q = 1000$ (extrapolated),
the advantage would grow to $\sim 10\times$.

These advantages compose with the Pauli term count reduction: fewer
terms means fewer gates per Trotter step, and lower 1-norm means
fewer steps.  The product $N_{\mathrm{Pauli}} \times r$ determines
the total gate budget, and both factors are simultaneously reduced
by the GeoVac encoding.

\subsection{Context: fault-tolerant Gaussian compression methods}
\label{sec:ft_gaussian}

The fault-tolerant QPE literature employs block-encoding methods---double
factorization (DF)~\cite{motta2021}, tensor hypercontraction
(THC)~\cite{lee2021}, and symmetry-compressed double factorization
(SCDF)~\cite{rocca2024}---that do not produce Pauli decompositions.
These methods target the block-encoding 1-norm~$\lambda$ and Toffoli
gate count rather than Pauli term count, and are implemented as
sequences of rotations within a linear combination of unitaries (LCU)
circuit.  Published benchmarks focus on large active spaces: FeMoCo at
108--152~qubits~\cite{lee2021}, cytochrome~P450 at
96--116~qubits~\cite{caesura2025}, and similar production-scale systems.
To our knowledge, no published DF, THC, or SCDF $\lambda$~values exist
for molecules at the 10--70~qubit operating scale of the present work
(LiH at $Q = 30$, H$_2$O at $Q = 70$), because these systems are too
small for the overhead of block-encoding to be beneficial.
The Pauli term advantage reported here (190$\times$--1,712$\times$,
Sec.~\ref{sec:composed_gaussian}) is therefore against raw
Jordan--Wigner encoding, the representation used by VQE and other
near-term variational algorithms.  The 1-norm comparison
(Sec.~\ref{sec:onenorm}) and its scaling ($\lambda \sim Q^{1.69}$)
are the metrics relevant for both VQE shot budgets and fault-tolerant
qubitization query complexity.

\paragraph{Computed 1-norm comparison.}
To quantify the 1-norm comparison directly, we compute Gaussian raw
Jordan--Wigner baselines from published molecular integrals.
For LiH~STO-3G (6~spatial orbitals, $Q = 12$, FCI $E = -7.881$~Ha),
the raw JW Hamiltonian has 907~Pauli terms, $\lambda = 34.3$~Ha, and
273~QWC groups.  The composed GeoVac LiH ($Q = 30$, electronic-only
with PK partitioned classically) has 334~Pauli terms,
$\lambda = 33.3$~Ha, and 21~QWC groups.  At comparable accuracy
($\sim$5\% $R_\mathrm{eq}$ error for both minimal-basis representations),
GeoVac achieves 2.7$\times$ fewer Pauli terms and 13$\times$ fewer
QWC groups, with essentially identical 1-norm ($0.97\times$).
The 1-norm parity reflects that $\lambda$ is dominated by one-body
terms (core energies, PK), which scale with nuclear charge rather than
orbital count.  The Pauli and QWC advantage are the VQE-relevant
metrics.  For the validated He equal-qubit comparison at $Q = 28$,
the GeoVac lattice has $6.8\times$ lower 1-norm than Gaussian
cc-pVTZ ($\lambda = 78.4$ vs.\ $530.5$~Ha), demonstrating that the
1-norm advantage grows with basis size for single-center systems.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Several caveats apply to the present results.

\emph{System size.}  The scaling exponents are extracted from four data
points at $Q = 10$--$110$ qubits.  While the $1/M^2$ ERI density decay
has an analytical foundation in the Gaunt integral selection rules, the
precise exponents ($\alpha_{\mathrm{terms}} = 3.15$,
$\alpha_{\lambda} = 1.69$) may shift slightly as larger systems (Li, Be,
molecular H$_2$ at higher $n_{\max}$) are included.  All $R^2$~values
exceed 0.997, indicating good power-law behavior over the range tested.

\emph{Gaussian baseline.}  The genuine Gaussian integrals
(Sec.~\ref{sec:genuine_gaussian}) now cover He at three basis sizes
(STO-3G, cc-pVDZ, cc-pVTZ), providing validated equal-qubit
comparisons at $Q = 2, 10, 28$.  At the two largest sizes, the
GeoVac lattice produces $1.3\times$ and $8.1\times$ fewer Pauli
terms with $3.8\times$ and $6.8\times$ lower 1-norm, respectively.
The 6-31G entry in Table~\ref{tab:pauli} still uses synthetic
integrals with representative sparsity.  The molecular Gaussian
baselines rely on published Pauli counts from
Trenev~\textit{et al.}~\cite{trenev2025}.  The structural
conclusion---Gaussian ERIs are near-dense while lattice ERIs
are selection-rule-sparse---is robust and well
established~\cite{helgaker2000}.

\emph{1-norm bound.}  The Trotter step counts in Table~\ref{tab:onenorm}
use the standard $\lambda^2$ upper bound (Childs et al.~\cite{childs2021},
Eq.~48).  As shown in Table~\ref{tab:comm_bound}, commutator-based bounds
that exploit the detailed Hamiltonian structure yield $7\times$ fewer Trotter
steps at $Q = 60$, with scaling exponent $\alpha = 1.47$ versus~$1.69$.
The commutator sweep covers $n_{\max} = 2$--$4$ (three data points);
the $n_{\max} = 5$ system ($N = 227{,}338$ terms) requires
$\sim\!52$~GB for the $N \times N$ anticommutativity matrix and was
not computed.  The scaling exponent may shift slightly with additional data
points.

\emph{Orbital ordering.}  Jordan--Wigner Pauli weights depend on the
spin-orbital ordering.  We use the natural lattice ordering
$(n, l, m, \sigma)$ sorted lexicographically.  Optimized orderings (e.g.,
interleaving by angular momentum) could further reduce the average Pauli
weight without changing the term count.

\emph{Beyond two-electron integrals.}  For systems with strong
relativistic effects or explicit correlation factors, additional integral
classes arise that are not captured by the present analysis.  The selection
rule argument applies specifically to the nonrelativistic Coulomb ERI
tensor.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ========================================================================

We have demonstrated that qubit Hamiltonians constructed from spectral
graph theory lattices produce structurally sparse Pauli decompositions
across three operationally relevant cost metrics:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Pauli term count:} $O(Q^{3.15})$, a $Q^{1.0\text{--}1.5}$-fold
  improvement over the $O(Q^{3.9\text{--}4.3})$ scaling of published
  Gaussian-basis encodings~\cite{trenev2025}.
\item \textbf{QWC measurement groups:} $O(Q^{3.36})$, meaning the
  shot budget for variational algorithms scales comparably to the
  term count.
\item \textbf{Pauli 1-norm:} $O(Q^{1.69})$, the key result for
  fault-tolerant simulation.  Because the 1-norm controls both
  Trotter step counts and qubitization query complexity, this
  sub-quadratic scaling implies that the GeoVac lattice basis
  produces Hamiltonians that are not just sparse in structure but
  \emph{concentrated in weight}.
\end{itemize}
The advantage is structural: angular momentum selection rules in the
Gaunt integrals enforce a $\sim 1/M^2$ ERI density, and the surviving
integrals are dominated by a small number of large diagonal
contributions.  Equal-qubit comparisons against validated Gaussian
cc-pVDZ and cc-pVTZ integrals confirm the advantage at the 1-norm
level: $6.8\times$ lower $\lambda$ at $Q = 28$, translating directly
into fewer Trotter steps.  This sparsity is a \emph{basis property},
present before any qubit tapering, Pauli grouping, or tensor
factorization---and compatible with all of these downstream
optimizations (Sec.~\ref{sec:composability}).

For quantum chemistry on quantum hardware, where circuit depth and
measurement budget are the primary constraints, the choice of
second-quantized basis can be as consequential as the choice of quantum
algorithm.  The GeoVac lattice Hamiltonian, with its $O(N)$ classical
construction cost~\cite{loutey_paper7}, $O(Q^{3.15})$ Pauli encoding,
and $O(Q^{1.69})$ simulation-cost scaling, provides a favorable
starting point for both NISQ variational methods and fault-tolerant
Hamiltonian simulation.  For composed multi-center systems (Sec.~\ref{sec:composed}), the
pseudopotential approximation yields block-diagonal ERIs with a
universal Pauli scaling of $O(Q^{2.5})$, independent of molecular
topology: LiH ($\alpha = 2.50$), BeH$_2$ ($\alpha = 2.51$), and
H$_2$O ($\alpha = 2.52$) all fall within an exponent spread of~0.02.
The advantage over published Gaussian baselines~\cite{trenev2025}
grows with system size: at $Q = 196$, composed H$_2$O requires
$\sim 1{,}700\times$ fewer Pauli terms than the Gaussian power-law
interpolation.  The exponent gap of $\sim 1.5$ between $Q^{2.5}$
and $Q^{3.9\text{--}4.3}$ implies that this advantage is structural
and persistent.  The composition
architecture~\cite{loutey_paper17}---which decomposes each molecule
into a fiber bundle of single-center orbital blocks---provides a
concrete path to polyatomic systems: each additional atomic center
adds an independent ERI block without creating cross-block terms,
provided the pseudopotential approximation remains valid.

Software implementing the encoder
(\texttt{geovac.qubit\_encoding}), measurement grouping
(\texttt{geovac.measurement\_grouping}), and Trotter analysis
(\texttt{geovac.trotter\_bounds}), together with all benchmarks
reported here, are available as part of the open-source GeoVac package.

\paragraph{Note added v2.0.24.}
The full $N$-electron quantum encoding comparison (Track~AS,
v2.0.23) has confirmed that the composed architecture is categorically
sparser than direct grid encoding of the exact 4-electron angular
Hamiltonian: at $Q = 10$, the full Jordan--Wigner encoding produces
3{,}288~Pauli terms with a 1-norm of 739~Ha, compared to
334~terms and 37~Ha for the composed encoding at $Q = 30$.
The root cause is structural: the full encoding maps finite-difference
grid points in the 11-dimensional angular space (dense FD coupling),
while the composed encoding maps orbital occupations with
block-diagonal ERIs from Gaunt selection rules.  Cross-pair $V_{ee}$
costs more in Pauli terms than the PK/$Z_{\rm eff}$/exchange overhead
it replaces.  This validates the composed architecture as the quantum
computing approach for multi-electron molecules.

\paragraph{Note added v2.0.44.}
Paper~20 presents the GeoVac Hamiltonian library as a practical tool
for the quantum computing community, including FCI accuracy benchmarks
across the LiH potential energy surface, matched-accuracy resource
comparisons against Gaussian baselines, and ecosystem export to
Qiskit, OpenFermion, and PennyLane.  The balanced coupled architecture
(Paper~19) achieves 0.20\% energy error at FCI, demonstrating that
the composed partition Hamiltonian is accurate when solved exactly.

\paragraph{Note added v2.4.0.}
The molecule library now spans 30 systems ($Z = 1$--36), including
two transition metal hydrides: ScH ($Z = 21$, [Ar] frozen core) and
TiH ($Z = 22$, [Ar] frozen core).  Both use isolated $d$-orbital
valence blocks ($l_{\min} = l_{\max} = 2$) with ERI density of 4.0\%,
half that of $s$+$p$ blocks (8.9\%).  The resulting Pauli/$Q$
ratio of~9.27 falls below the universal coefficient of~11.11,
demonstrating that $d$-electron systems are \emph{cheaper} to encode
than main-group molecules at the same qubit count.  Transition metals
are reclassified from ``out of scope'' to ``scoping demonstrated.''
