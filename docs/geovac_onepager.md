# GeoVac: 51-1,712x Fewer Pauli Terms for Molecular Quantum Simulation

I built a framework that produces qubit Hamiltonians with 51x-1,712x fewer Pauli terms than standard Gaussian-basis Jordan-Wigner encodings for molecular quantum simulation. The library covers **30 molecules** across three periodic table rows, including multi-center systems (CO, N2, F2, CH2O, C2H2, C2H6) and transition metal hydrides (ScH, TiH).

The concrete result: a lithium hydride Hamiltonian on 30 qubits with 334 Pauli terms, where a comparable Gaussian cc-pVDZ encoding produces roughly 63,500. For water on 70 qubits, it's 778 terms versus an estimated 1.33M. The scaling exponent is **Q^2.5** across all molecules tested (universal coefficient: N_Pauli = 11.11 x Q), compared to Q^3.9-4.3 for Gaussian Jordan-Wigner. The head-to-head 1-norm comparison against computed Gaussian baselines shows GeoVac LiH matches STO-3G (33.3 vs 34.3 Ha) with 2.7x fewer Pauli terms and 13x fewer QWC measurement groups.

The sparsity is structural, not approximate. GeoVac assigns each electron group in a molecule -- core pairs, bond pairs, lone pairs -- its own natural coordinate system (hyperspherical or prolate spheroidal), then couples them through screened Coulomb integrals. Angular momentum selection rules enforced by the Gaunt integrals produce exact zeros in the electron repulsion integrals, giving block-diagonal two-electron terms with no cross-block entries. This structure survives Jordan-Wigner encoding and is compatible with all downstream optimizations.

For near-term quantum computing, this matters because the measurement budget in VQE scales with the number of Pauli terms. Fewer terms means proportionally fewer measurements to estimate the energy. The 1-norm (which controls shot noise and Trotter error) scales as Q^1.69.

I want to be upfront about limitations. GeoVac's classical accuracy is below production quantum chemistry -- 5% equilibrium bond length error for LiH, 12% for BeH2, 26% for water, compared to sub-0.1% for Gaussian methods. The accuracy is best suited for single-point quantum simulation at fixed geometries (0.20% energy error for LiH at n_max=3), not geometry optimization. The Pauli advantage is against raw Jordan-Wigner -- density fitting and tensor hypercontraction use block-encoding representations that bypass the Pauli picture entirely, so the comparison is specifically relevant to VQE and other variational near-term methods. For atoms, the framework achieves 0.004% He accuracy (2D variational solver + cusp correction, zero free parameters).

The Hamiltonians are available as a standalone Python package (`pip install geovac-hamiltonians`) with export to OpenFermion, Qiskit, and PennyLane. The underlying theory is documented in a 21-paper series on GitHub with DOI-stamped Zenodo releases.
